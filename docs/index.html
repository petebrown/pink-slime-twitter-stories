As midterms approach, old practices persist and new trends emerge across the country's biggest "pink slime" news network

We found over 12,000 instances of politicians' tweets being published verbatim as "news" articles

By Pete Brown

August 7, 2022

Metric Media bills its network of "more than 1,300 community news sites" as an antidote to the nation's local news crisis.

A description on the Cincy Reporter, which is part of the Ohio Network, states, "Metric Media was established to fill the void in community news after years of decline in local reporting by legacy media. This site is one of hundreds nationwide to inform citizens about news in their local communities."

These are admirable goals, but the mysterious network has faced extensive criticism for its shunning of journalistic norms and other questionable practices since it was exposed in the run-up to the 2020 election.

NewsGuard summarizes Metric Media as a "network of websites that falsely present themselves as locally based news sites. The sites do not disclose their conservative agenda, and much of the content is created by algorithms."

This reliance on low-cost automated has seen the Metric Media become synonymous with so-called "pink slime" journalism. The New York Times described it as being “rooted in deception, eschewing hallmarks of news reporting like fairness and transparency” and alleged a pay-for-play operation through which public figures could pay for flattering coverage.

But what is happening on these sites as we creep ever closer to the 2022 primaries? We analyzed 156 Metric Media sites in five states to find out.

The Cincy Reporter seems very fond of Jenn Giroux

We scraped records of the 6,722 articles that appeared on the frontpages of the 156 sites in Metric Media's networks in Arizona, Florida, Georgia, Ohio and West Virginia.

Named entity recognition enabled us to identify people whose names appeared in headlines.

At the highest level, the findings initially appear somewhat predictable and unremarkable -- "Biden" was most common (26 instances), followed by "Trump" (21).

At the level of individual sites, however, the data is considerably more revealing, suggesting that the network still has a penchant for boosting certain political candidates under the guise of news.

For example, ten of the 48 articles on the Cincy Reporter homepage had headlines mentioning "[Jenn] Giroux", a Republican candidate described as "a mother of nine who is running for the State House in the 27th district."

All ten -- nine of which could be described as being above the fold -- promoted Giroux policy positions and talking points through headlines such as 'Giroux supports 'Backpack Bill': "The education tax dollars follows the kid with the backpack"' and 'Giroux opposes Leftist indoctrination of children of all ages: "Teachers need to know there are boundaries that you do not cross"'.

Nine stories mentioning Jenn Giroux on the Cincy Reporter homepage.

Tweets as news

Our analysis inadvertently uncovered a new technique these sites are using to populate their pages: Publishing politicians' tweets as regular news articles.

Headlines of these boilerplate stories all follow the same format: [State] U.S. Rep [Politician name]: "[Beginning of tweet content]". Regardless of their placement on the homepage, the stories themselves are tagged as "Local Government" with the outlet's name as the byline.

A Rome Reporter 'news' article derived from tweets by Majorie Taylor Greene.

We initially found 166 instances of these stories across 58 of the sites we analyzed.

Five such examples appeared on the front-page of the Rome Reporter, all regurgitating tweets from Majorie Taylor Greene (R-GA 14th District). Three appeared under the Data Points section and two were listed under the Local Government header in the News section.

Headlines under the "Data Points" section of the Rome Reporter.

Digging deeper, it becomes clear that these articles have been a mainstay of Metric Media sites for around five months.

We found 12,036 articles containing nothing more than politicians' tweets across 58 sites.

The Arizona-based NW Valley Times has published 604 articles derived from tweets by Representative Debbie Lesko (R-AZ 8th District) since March. Similarly, the Rome Reporter has pushed 568 articles from Majorie Taylor Greene's tweets.

Some of the 604 articles the NW Valley Times has generated from tweets by Rep. Debbie Lesko.

Quite how such output fits Metric Media's claim to "provide 100% original reporting" is unclear.
